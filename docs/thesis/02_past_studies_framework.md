# 2.0 Past Studies and Proposed Framework

This chapter reviews the literature on seismic risk in Metro Manila and extracts key elements that inform the thesis methodology. It discusses limitations of prior studies before surveying current practice in performance-based earthquake engineering and portfolio loss estimation. The proposed framework is then presented, addressing gaps in past research by bridging high-resolution building-specific seismic risk assessments with macroscopic portfolio loss estimation approaches.

## 2.1 Past Studies on Seismic Risk in Metro Manila

### 2.1.1 MMEIRS and GMMA-RAP

Between 2002 and 2004, the Japanese International Cooperation Agency (JICA) and the Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) published the Metro Manila Earthquake Impact Reduction Study (MMEIRS), designed to provide municipalities and citizens with tools to respond to earthquakes and associated hazards including fire-following earthquake, liquefaction, and tsunami scenarios. MMEIRS provided critical earthquake faulting scenarios used in this thesis, most notably the West Valley Fault (WVF), which cuts directly through the capital region. This foundational study illuminated the seismic dangers facing Metro Manila and enabled future research, though it suffered from limitations in its proposed building damage functions (fragilities).

Nearly a decade later, PHIVOLCS and Geoscience Australia released the Greater Metropolitan Manila Area—Risk Assessment Project (GMMA-RAP), a companion risk assessment that leveraged high-resolution digital elevation data and imagery to create a more accurate exposure database. This enabled more refined studies of flood risk, tropical cyclone and severe wind exposure, and updated earthquake risk analysis.

The GMMA-RAP incorporated simplified fragility and vulnerability curves for local building types as functions of peak ground acceleration (PGA), developed through a combination of analytical methods (non-linear static pushover analysis), empirical approaches (post-earthquake visual surveys and damage reports), and heuristic expert judgment. These curves were developed by the University of the Philippines Diliman—Institute for Civil Engineering (UPD-ICE). Collapse rates and corresponding casualty models were based on the simplified approach outlined in FEMA-HAZUS (2001).

Improving upon MMEIRS methods, the GMMA-RAP team selected existing Ground Motion Prediction Models (GMPM/GMPEs) to characterize regional seismicity using residual analyses of 18 seismic events ranging from magnitude 4.9 to 5. A critical limitation is the lack of strong-motion recordings in the Philippines and the absence of Philippine-specific GMPEs. The WVF magnitude 7.2 rupture scenario from GMMA-RAP shows expected building collapses concentrated in Makati and Quezon City.

MMEIRS and GMMA-RAP provided vital foundational information for defining seismic hazard and exposure in later chapters of this thesis. The fragilities developed for GMMA-RAP were adapted to create simplified models of secondary building archetypes.

### 2.1.2 World Bank and Miyamoto Study on Metro Manila Schools

The World Bank (2014) released a report on Safe and Resilient Infrastructure in the Philippines, which highlighted a vulnerability assessment of 3,821 public school buildings in Metro Manila conducted by Miyamoto International. The study applied FEMA-HAZUS fragilities for the most common structural system—low- and mid-rise reinforced-concrete moment frames (RCMRFs, or C1-L/C1-M in HAZUS classification).

Gilani and Miyamoto (2017) found that approximately 50% of Metro Manila's school buildings—housing 2.15 million students—were categorized as "high-risk," indicating susceptibility to significant damage or collapse during a design-level seismic event. A design-level earthquake in this scenario would likely result in approximately 24,400 student fatalities, with projected total loss of US$820 million, representing more than 80% of the total portfolio replacement value (US$1 billion). A cost-benefit analysis showed that retrofitting approximately 5% of the building stock (186 buildings) at a cost of US$40–80 million could reduce student fatalities by 25%. Invitations for consulting and construction services for retrofitting government buildings and schools were issued throughout 2018–2019. Miyamoto et al. (2014) also prepared retrofitting design guidelines for typical school and hospital buildings in the nation.

### 2.1.3 Limitations of Past Studies

The Gilani and Miyamoto (2017) study was primarily constrained by its exclusive reliance on American-based HAZUS methodology, which did not account for archetypes beyond reinforced-concrete moment frames. The prioritization of schools for retrofitting was driven primarily by occupant numbers, general regional soil classification, and distance to faults. Direct non-linear modeling was not employed; instead, benefits of structural upgrading were assessed by reclassifying "Low-Code" building fragilities to "High-Code" equivalents. Similarly, the fragilities used in GMMA-RAP—though Philippine-specific and derived from both empirical and analytical methods—did not fully leverage detailed non-linear modeling and time-history analysis. The effects of specific ground-motion characteristics were not examined beyond stochastic demands generated from GMPEs, and near-fault effects of the WVF were merely addressed through a "near-fault factor" applied to code-based design spectra.

Both studies relied on simplified HAZUS damage indices, roughly equating fragility damage states with loss ratios. Non-structural component performance was not included directly. This lack of resolution limited the accuracy and utility of these studies for detailed decision-making on risk mitigation for individual portfolios.

## 2.2 State of Practice: Building Portfolio Loss Estimation

Previous Philippine seismic risk studies adopted macroscopic approaches (primarily HAZUS) because of their ability to provide rapid loss estimates across very large building portfolios using coarse-grained exposure databases—for example, entire city building stocks. However, at the individual building level, these analyses rely on simplified single-degree-of-freedom structures intended to represent entire classes of buildings with tremendous internal variability. This approach lacks the resolution necessary to identify specific risk mitigation strategies for owners managing large portfolios of tens to hundreds of similar buildings.

In contrast, probabilistic building-specific earthquake risk assessments based on the PEER PBEE (Pacific Earthquake Engineering Research) framework, as implemented in FEMA P-58 (2012) and the USRC (2015) rating system, can incorporate detailed multi-degree-of-freedom (MDOF) non-linear response history analysis (NRHA) results. Compared to macroscopic approaches, this methodology better captures vulnerabilities relevant to asset management and owner decision-making. The trade-off is computational and financial intensity—applying this framework across many buildings is often neither time-efficient nor economically feasible.

Recent research has worked to address this efficiency limitation by using NRHA results to develop detailed fragility and vulnerability functions for specific building portfolios. Examples include Yoshikawa and Goda (2014), who assessed a 4,000-building portfolio of wood-frame houses in British Columbia using the UBC-SAWS model and incorporated spatially-correlated seismic demands. Lamego et al. (2017) analyzed approximately 2,000 reinforced-concrete and masonry buildings in Lisbon using fragilities derived from detailed non-linear static models and target response spectra. Burton et al. (2015) applied FEMA P-58 methodology to develop multi-building community-level resilience functions. Ottonelli et al. (2017) and O'Reilly et al. (2018) integrated multiple-stripe analysis (MSA) NRHA with region-specific repair costs for structural and non-structural components. Zeng et al. (2016) employed multi-story concentrated-mass shear (MCS) models and direct non-linear time-history analysis to derive engineering demand parameters (EDPs) for determining P-58 losses in a 619-building portfolio at Tsinghua University, China, under three ground-shaking intensities. That methodology was extended by Lu et al. (2020) through the NHERI SimCenter workflow to assess 1.8 million buildings in San Francisco using HAZUS-based MCS models assembled from geographic information system data and spatially varying ground motion.

However, past studies have not explicitly evaluated probabilistic recovery of the full building stock or the benefits of mitigation efforts. This is a significant gap because, for many infrastructure portfolios, financial loss alone is insufficient as a performance metric. For example, Haiti's airport, seaports, and most paved roads were severely damaged in the 2010 earthquake, disrupting the flow of relief goods. Salazar et al. (2016) documented acute illness following earthquakes and severe weather in the Philippines due to inadequate sanitation, overstressed healthcare systems, and overcrowded temporary shelters. Cascading effects also occur in developed nations: the 2011 Christchurch earthquake in New Zealand caused non-structural damage to healthcare facilities that, despite minor structural impact, reduced bed capacity and forced subcontracting of services for weeks to months.

A resilience-based approach enables stakeholders to address economic and social impacts beyond immediate damage, casualties, and monetary losses, considering recovery trajectories and functional restoration. Arup (2017) demonstrated this broader perspective in an ongoing project to improve seismic resilience of a 328-building campus portfolio at the University of British Columbia. They combined FEMA P-154 rapid visual screening with quantitative collapse risk assessments from NRHAs; developed detailed 3D models for three archetypal buildings; calibrated representative simplified-MDOFs; generated EDPs through NRHA; performed Monte Carlo analysis across four seismic intensities to estimate P-58 monetary losses and repair times; and applied the Arup REDi framework for downtime estimation. Conceptual structural and non-structural retrofit schemes were evaluated through cost-benefit analysis, and overall campus infrastructure resilience was investigated.

## 2.3 Proposed Framework

This thesis proposes a framework leveraging building-specific vulnerability functions derived from detailed 3D non-linear models of key building archetypes, simplified SDOF/MDOF models for secondary archetypes, and Philippine-specific FEMA P-58 (2018)–based repair cost and time consequence functions. Probabilistic distributions of financial losses, casualties, and recovery time are generated conditional on seismic intensity. Sampling archetype performance across a range of intensities enables analysis of large portfolio seismic resilience under stochastic fault scenarios with reduced computational effort while utilizing high-performance computing for efficient Monte Carlo simulations.

Significant effort was devoted to surveying and characterizing local building practices and cost trends to overcome the obstacle of applying North American FEMA P-58 methodology to Philippine conditions. While the structural and non-structural consequence functions presented in this thesis remain early-stage, they serve as a benchmark for PBEE loss estimation in the Philippines. The work also establishes a ground-motion simulation and non-linear structural modeling framework accessible to diverse practicing engineers in the Philippines and other developing countries.

The framework comprises six primary components—(i) Stakeholder Engagement, (ii) Hazard, (iii) Exposure, (iv) Fragility, (v) Vulnerability, and (vi) Risk—that function as modules flowing from one to another and can be iterated as needed.

Stakeholders with interest in seismic resilience of distributed infrastructure portfolios in the Metro Manila region were engaged as part of project launch. Through the Earthquakes and Megacities Initiative (EMI) and the Federation of Indian Chambers of Commerce Philippines Inc., the Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Offices (DRRMOs) of Makati and Quezon City were engaged in mid-2017. DRRMO representatives proposed focusing on seismic resilience of public-school building portfolios. Other key stakeholders included city departments and national-level organizations: the Philippine Department of Education (DepEd), Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH), the Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS), and the Department of the Interior and Local Governments (DILG).

The seismic hazard across the entire Metro Manila region was investigated to provide ground-motion information for building response estimation and earthquake scenario simulation. In parallel, the school building exposure database was assembled and assessed through rapid visual screening following FEMA P-154 (2015) and additional data collection.

Portfolio buildings were categorized into archetypes, and detailed models were developed in PERFORM-3D following ASCE-41 (2017) or simplified methods. Building responses were simulated under a range of seismic intensities and selected ground motions. Building fragilities were paired with newly developed Philippine-specific FEMA P-58–derived component consequence functions and the Arup REDi downtime estimation framework, generating probabilistic archetype vulnerabilities through P-58–based Monte Carlo simulations.

Finally, thousands of Monte Carlo realizations of governing earthquake scenarios were simulated to sample vulnerabilities and determine portfolio-level losses and to examine preliminary life-safety mitigation strategies. The project scope and task breakdown by module is summarized as follows:

**Seismic Hazard:** Characterization and synthesis of up-to-date earthquake scenarios for the Metro Manila region; development of earthquake record suite incorporating near-fault effects at multiple intensities.

**Exposure:** Urban context assessment; inventory collection for a >1,000-building portfolio; rapid visual screening of ~200 buildings across target cities; collection of structural, architectural, plumbing, and electrical drawings and building plans; grouping buildings into key archetypes; re-design of archetypes lacking available drawings.

**Fragility—Non-linear Response History Analysis:** Non-linear response history analysis for four primary archetypes and conceptual retrofit; adaptation of simplified modeling methods for other archetypes.

**Vulnerability—Archetype-Specific Loss Estimation:** Adaptation of FEMA P-58 compatible fragilities and development of Philippine-specific damage consequence functions for structural and non-structural portfolio components; simulation of archetype-specific monetary loss, casualties, and downtime across earthquake intensity ranges; development of normalized archetype vulnerabilities.

**Risk—Portfolio Loss Estimation:** Probabilistic simulation of five governing earthquake scenarios for Metro Manila; assessment of portfolio-level monetary loss, casualties, and downtime across two cities; evaluation of proposed mitigation strategy impacts.

The following chapters explore the high-level seismic hazard and exposure database of the study region. Once key building archetypes are established, efforts concentrate on seismic structural responses and resulting losses, ultimately enabling high-resolution evaluation of portfolio-wide losses.
