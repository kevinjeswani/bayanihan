# 1.0 Introduction

## Geographic & Seismic Context

The Philippines, an archipelago nation of over 105 million people, sits in a tectonically active region between the Philippine Sea Plate and the Sunda Plate. In the past 30 years, this area has experienced 125 seismic events of magnitude 6.0 or greater, including 20 events of magnitude 7.0 or above.

Metro Manila, the nation's capital, is transected by the West Valley Fault (WVF) and lies adjacent to the sub-parallel East Valley Fault (EVF). Both faults total 90 to 135 km in length. The WVF has an estimated return period of 400–600 years with potential magnitudes between 6.5 and 7.2, based on recent surface-faulting trenching activities and radiocarbon dating. The last known event occurred in 1658, suggesting a catastrophic earthquake is imminent. Little is known about the hazards posed by the EVF, further complicating the threat assessment.

## Economic Criticality of Metro Manila

Metro Manila is a densely populated region with 13 million people, 1.3 million of whom live in informal settlements. The metropolitan area comprises one municipality and 16 highly urbanized cities, representing half of the nation's total HUCs. Despite housing only 12% of the nation's population, Metro Manila accounts for 38% of the nation's gross domestic product. No other regional hub could easily replace its economic and governance structures.

This signals an urgent need for a tractable methodology to assess and improve the resilience of Metro Manila's building structures to support long-term sustainability. Existing frameworks are either too coarse-grained to capture necessary details for understanding natural disaster risk, or too specific and costly for implementation in a developing country context.

## Thesis Scope & Contribution

This thesis proposes a seismic risk planning framework for assessing and improving the resilience of a spatially distributed building portfolio of over 1,000 public school buildings. These schools currently serve over 560,000 students across two cities in Metro Manila:

- **Makati City**: 96 buildings across 40 elementary/secondary school campuses, one university campus, and one DepEd office, distributed across 26 km
- **Quezon City**: Approximately 925 buildings across 142 schools, distributed across 165 km

The proposed framework provides meaningful insight into monetary losses, casualties, and recovery times by adapting state-of-the-art North American methodologies to the Philippine context.

## 1.1 Threats of Natural Disasters to Metro Manila

### Recent Earthquake Events

Between October 16–31, 2019, successive earthquakes (magnitude 6.4 to 6.6) struck in and near Tulunan, North Cotabato, Mindanao, close to Metro Davao. The damage was extensive:
- Displaced over 60,000 residents
- Damaged around 50,000 building structures
- Disrupted nearly 3.4 million students through the functional loss of over 1,500 schools

Two months later, on December 15, 2019, a magnitude 6.8 earthquake struck 25 kilometers away in Matanao, Davao del Sur, resulting in partial damage to an additional 327 schools. These events occurred in regions less densely populated than Metro Manila; a similar event in the nation's capital would likely result in significant devastation.

### Recurring Natural Disasters

While Metro Manila has not been exposed to major earthquakes in recent years, it is no stranger to major natural disasters. In 2009, typhoon Ketsana caused significant damage amounting to PHP 4.3 billion (approximately US$127 million in 2020) to building infrastructure, including 1,382 public schools. This magnitude of devastation is a common occurrence in the Philippines and poses a significant threat to the resilience of the Filipino people and their infrastructure systems.

### Critical Role of School Buildings

Public school buildings in the Philippines are designated as emergency shelters. However, in the case of the Mindanao earthquakes, this vital infrastructure system was not available to receive local inhabitants. Given that 16.6% of the population lives below the national poverty line, disaster resilience and safeguarding the educational system are critical to ensuring a sustainable and prosperous future in the Philippines.

### Recent Policy & Investment Initiatives

The World Bank recently assisted the Philippine Department of Education (DepEd) in conducting a high-level seismic risk assessment of its Metro Manila school portfolio and the potential threats posed to students. In addition to initiating a Metro Manila school building retrofit program, the nation has secured US$225 million in catastrophe bonds for the next three years to support this work. However, deeper focus on the recovery of the school portfolio as a function of time following a potential earthquake and its implications for disaster rescue has not yet been studied.

## 1.2 Limitations of Existing Portfolio Risk Frameworks

### High-Level Regional Approaches

Several well-regarded high-level methodologies—such as FEMA-HAZUS (2001), proprietary risk models by RMS (2020), and AIR (2020)—are currently used to address seismic risk in large regions like Metro Manila. However, these methodologies have limitations in modeling "on-the-ground" realities of large metropolitan areas where resources (financial, time, personnel) are quite limited. They are often based on generalized building fragilities and vulnerabilities but do not provide building-portfolio owners with a more surgical toolkit to understand and increase their seismic resilience.

### Building-Specific Performance-Based Approaches

During the same period, significant advances have been made in building-specific performance-based earthquake engineering (PBEE), particularly with detailed non-linear dynamic structural analysis. These improvements have allowed structural engineers to better grasp building behavior, enabling the design of increasingly complex structures. However, these improvements alone do not provide end-users with useful decision-making metrics for resilience.

### Loss Estimation Methods

Progress in this domain has been more recently achieved through building-specific loss estimation procedures such as FEMA P-58 (2012a) and Arup REDi (Almufti & Willford, 2013). These methods use accurate simulated building response data to probabilistically assess damage to different structural and non-structural components throughout a building. Damage to specific components, resulting casualties, required repair costs, and different recovery time metrics can thereby be determined. However, these loss estimation procedures were developed for the North American context.

## 1.3 Thesis Contributions

A hybrid framework that adapts state-of-the-art building-specific methods for the Philippine context and applies portfolio risk analysis methods was previously developed by Guo et al. (2018) and Jeswani et al. (2020). This thesis presents the significant effort that went into compiling and adapting Philippine-specific structural component fragilities and associated consequence functions, allowing the P-58 method and REDi method to be more appropriately applied to the Metro Manila public school building portfolio.

This framework can be thought of as an amalgamation of high-level catastrophe models and more advanced seismic engineering analysis. Detailed structural models feed into Philippine-specific loss estimation methodology to provide meaningful insight on how to apply simple non-structural mitigation schemes to supplement structural retrofitting. The tool ultimately provides a foundation for local practitioners to assess the resilience of new buildings in the design phase and improve the resilience of entire existing critical building portfolios.

## 1.4 Thesis Organization

**Chapter 2** describes key findings of existing studies on seismic risk in Metro Manila and their limitations, contrasting them with recent improvements in earthquake engineering. A high-level overview of the proposed framework is provided, showing how building-specific and regional seismic risk methods can be integrated.

**Chapter 3** describes the seismic hazard (ground-shaking) definition for the schools and develops ground motion suites by spectral matching procedures considering near-fault conditions.

**Chapter 4** defines the exposure database by presenting an overview of the urban context, common building typologies in Metro Manila, and typical structural deficiencies noted after major earthquakes in the Philippines. An in-depth review of data collection methods and building-into-archetype grouping based on rapid visual screening and vintage structural code analysis is provided.

**Chapter 5** details the methodology for non-linear time history analyses of selected building archetypes. Structural response parameters, typical damage, and collapse modes are discussed. The development of simplified models for secondary archetypes is also presented.

**Chapter 6** illustrates the Philippine-specific P-58-based repair cost and time consequence functions for structural and non-structural contents. The methodology for building-specific time-based analysis is outlined, with the resulting performance of archetypes presented.

**Chapter 7** presents the overall portfolio risk analysis methodology and results for existing buildings. Simple mitigation measures are proposed and their effects on overall portfolio loss are examined to drive future stakeholder discussions.

**Chapter 8** concludes the thesis by summarizing the methodology and underscoring the benefits of this "hybrid" loss estimation approach for relatively large building portfolios. Limitations of the methods and possible research directions to improve the framework are described.
