# Chapter 8: Conclusion

## 8.0 Main Contribution and Overview

The main contribution of this thesis was the development of an integrated seismic resilience framework linking state-of-the-art building-specific earthquake engineering know-how and portfolio risk methodologies for evaluating moderately large building portfolios. Additionally, this framework was intended for use in developing nations since current industry frameworks generally reflect the North American and European contexts.

This thesis employed a case study approach and analyzed over 1,000 spatially distributed public-school buildings across Makati and Quezon City, Metro Manila, Philippines, serving approximately 500,000 students.

## 8.1 Summary of Key Accomplishments

### Building Response and Fragility Development

Detailed non-linear models of primary archetypes and a proposed retrofit of a non-ductile building were developed, while simplified methods were used for other archetypes. State-of-the-art seismic hazard definition tools were employed to define appropriate ground motions for the derivation of building responses over a range of intensities.

The Mid- and Low-Code NRHA archetypes were significantly impacted by weak-column-strong-beam mechanisms due to the influence of hollow block (CHB) infill walls. The beams of non-ductile RCMRF archetype suffered substantial shear degradation, while column shear hinges were engaged at higher intensities, thereby increasing the collapse risk. The proposed FRP retrofit of this non-ductile archetype almost doubled the median collapse capacity; however, the archetype still suffered significant residual drifts and upper-storey peak floor accelerations (PFAs) as was the case for the four existing primary archetypes. High residual drifts likely resulted from primarily pulse-like GM suites. The simplified models also experienced significant residual drifts and PFAs.

Local construction materials and typical structural deficiencies were captured using lumped-plasticity elements and shear hinges for non-ductile elements. Non-linear response history analyses (NRHA) were conducted to determine engineering demand parameters (EDPs) under each of the ten assembled GM suites, and collapse fragilities were established with an additional multiple stripes analysis. Three primary archetypes and five secondary archetypes were modelled through the FEMA P-58 (2018a) simplified methodology based on UPD-ICE general fragilities. While the simplified method may have limitations, it allowed for the incorporation of quick and reasonable response predictions into the portfolio assessment.

### Loss Estimation Framework

Building response simulations enabled the development of the archetypal vulnerability functions. To facilitate these building-specific loss assessments, fragilities were selected from existing studies to represent 31 structural and non-structural building components found in public schools and other Philippine government buildings. The adapted FEMA P-58 (2018a) loss estimation process incorporated Philippine-specific damage consequence functions developed in this project. Line-by-line cost estimates provided in the P-58 background documentation as a foundation for producing Philippine-specific repair cost and time consequence functions were used. A general cost conversion approach was employed to account for differences in material and labour costs between the Philippines and North America, while exclusions of specific repair tasks deemed inappropriate/unnecessary for Philippine public buildings were made. Local cost estimation guides and construction material prices were used to confirm and replace general conversions of repair activities, where possible. Mitigations were proposed for the most prevalent non-structural components to decrease injuries due to falling hazards.

Monte Carlo simulations following the P-58 framework were run for each archetype and for each intensity, while considering collapse cases and excessive residual drifts. Residual drifts and collapse risk account for most of the monetary losses with small contributions from repairs to CHB walls and structural repairs. Mean/median Loss ratios of most primary archetypes appeared to be quite low except for some total loss (replacement) cases at the highest intensities; this signals a need to revise the repair cost functions.

### Recovery Time Assessment

The REDi methodology was adopted for converting repair times into functional recovery downtime estimates, however results derived with default North American parameters need to be confirmed with post-disaster data in the Philippines. Downtimes appear to be primarily due to CHB wall repairs and financing delays with a small influence from structural repairs and collapse risk from the 475-year intensity onward. These downtime proportions may reflect the school building design—namely, their minimal non-structural components. However, total downtime values may be overestimated. The probabilistic distributions of losses were conditioned on spectral acceleration at the building's average fundamental period for each intensity. Each of the four decision variable (DV) distributions were discretized by decile and linearly interpolated and results across the five intensities were "stitched" together with a multiple stripes analysis to form loss "surfaces". In this manner, vulnerabilities can be assigned to each building in the portfolio before running a portfolio analysis.

### Portfolio Risk Analysis

Governing scenarios from important seismic generators were determined based on state-of-the-art probabilistic seismic hazard models. Monte Carlo simulations were employed in the portfolio risk analysis to generate thousands of realizations of stochastic rupture scenarios. Spatially correlated spectral acceleration values were determined at each site for each realization. A random sampling of a probability of non-exceedance along the normalized loss surface at the simulated spectral acceleration was conducted for each of the 1,021 buildings to determine its monetary losses, downtimes, and casualties based on its uniquely defined replacement cost/time and population. Individual building losses were then aggregated for the whole portfolio and probabilistic distributions of these results were then used to assess the proposed life-safety risk mitigation strategies. This allows decision variables to be determined in the portfolio analysis separately from archetype-specific loss estimation, alleviating the need to determine EDPs and damage states for every building in the portfolio.

### Key Results and Findings

Resulting median monetary loss ratios for individual cities under a M = 7.3 West Valley Fault rupture appeared to be quite low (0.26 − 0.31); however, no previous studies exist that would provide a meaningful comparison. A reasonable explanation for low monetary costs would be the bare nature of these public schools (i.e., minimal non-structural components). Furthermore, the accuracy of these results can be easily improved upon refining repair cost functions. Median 90% portfolio recovery times are around 640 days and 970 days for Quezon and Makati City, respectively, although subject to further revisions. This appears counterintuitive given that Makati post-tensioned moment-resisting reinforced concrete frames (PTMRFs) were assigned a higher residual drift limit than standard RCMRFs. However, damage to much larger Makati buildings and correspondingly high replacement times forced the portfolio recovery times upward. A two- to three-year portfolio recovery time is not unreasonable considering a maximum credible earthquake striking the region.

In contrast, the positive benefits of non-structural retrofits had a clear impact on decreasing median injury rates from 9.1% to 2.3% in Quezon City and 14.5% to 1.1% in Makati. By significantly decreasing the collapse risk of the non-ductile RCMRF through FRP retrofitting, fatality rates decreased modestly from 0.18% to 0.13% in Quezon City and from 0.3% to 0.04% in Makati. Additional research is required to optimize the benefits of seismic mitigation. The total estimated cost of structural mitigations is around 4.0% of replacement for Makati and 4.1% for Quezon City, while non-structural mitigations are estimated at 4.4% and 2.7%, respectively. This could possibly be reduced with cooperation from the DPWH and local design practitioners. These mitigation measures may be performed with the current World Bank school retrofit program to provide stakeholders with a well-rounded and effective risk-targeted effort.

### Framework Significance

This thesis aimed to address post-earthquake losses beyond direct consequences and to analyze the added dimension of recovery time, which is vital to determine and improve resilience in a more holistic manner. The "hybrid" loss estimation procedure outlined in the literature allows for detailed building response data to be generated for a small set of buildings, while assessing the seismic risk of an entire moderately large and spatially distributed portfolio. This work serves both as an advanced non-linear modelling guide for practitioners, but also lays some groundwork for Performance-Based Earthquake Engineering (PBEE) methodologies to be used more broadly in the Philippines. The intended audience for this framework are engineers in the Philippines, who may use and refine repair time and consequence functions and employ the FEMA P-58 PACT software. The proposed framework is intended to serve as a tractable solution to an increasingly more complex problem due to the cascading effects of climate change, rapid growth, and inequity.

## 8.2 Future Research

The work presented in this thesis is a small contribution towards a much broader problem which is to provide tractable solutions for developing countries in the area of infrastructure resilience to natural disasters. As such, significant consideration went into exploring avenues for future work.

### Seismic Hazard Definition

**Ground Motion Selection:** Possible influence of subcrustal ruptures at lower intensities requires obtaining detailed OpenQuake PSHA results and checking subcrustal contributions. Ground motions should be selected as necessary. Average directivity effects are too simplistic in determining amplification effects; appropriate GMPEs with directivity effects should be incorporated with each PSHA rupture. The possibility of incorporating directivity directly in portfolio analysis rupture realizations should also be investigated.

**Spectral Matching:** Spectral matching may introduce unintended changes in ground-motion characteristics. Investigation of conditional mean spectrum (CMS) approaches is recommended, as done in previous studies for Metro Manila with pulse-like ground motions. Amplitude-scaled ground motion alternatives should be investigated for comparison with spectrally-matched motions, and spectral matching should be updated to reflect accurate soil models.

**Pulse-Motions and Liquefaction:** Residual drifts may be attributed to the large proportion of pulse-like ground motions; sensitivity analysis with NRHA from non-pulse ground motions is needed. With 28 of 28 Makati sites on "moderate" to "high" liquefaction potential, more borehole logs should be collected to supplement existing data. Ground settlement was a major issue during the 2019 Mindanao Earthquake; total loss should be declared in portfolio analysis at a site if liquefaction is triggered by exceeding threshold peak ground acceleration.

### Building Fragility Functions

**Primary Archetypes:** Simplified models not accurate enough for some building types (C1-L (Pre/Lo), C1-H (Hi), S1-M (Hi)) require completion of NRHA. PTMRF models should be conducted with axial springs for post-tensioning to simulate tendon slip and rupture if possible; alternatively, at least confirmation that proxy-EDP assumptions are valid is required.

**Failure Modes and Modelling:** Investigation of shear failure modes if detailing is not implemented on site. Potential splice failure requires re-design and modelling with ASCE-41 recommendations. Joint confinement is potentially inadequate and requires investigation with updated shear backbone reinforcement and other modelling techniques. Bar slip and RC beam-column modelling may be too simplistic; more detailed mechanical models should be considered. Soil-structure interaction (SSI) should be considered if models of higher-rise buildings are developed; this requires more soil data.

**Retrofit Schemes:** FRP shear retrofitting may not adequately reduce drifts (both peak and residual). Testing of alternative schemes is proposed including shear/flexural retrofitting, reinforced concrete jacketing, buckling restrained brace (BRB) upgrades, reinforced concrete shear walls (RCSWs)/BRBs, and gapped-inclined-bracing solutions. Excessive residual drifts modelled through NRHA significantly impact losses; sensitivity studies with non-pulse motions and amplitude-scaled motions are needed.

**Additional Considerations:** Pounding is likely to be a severe issue, especially with buildings in Quezon City; gap elements should be incorporated in models. Vertical irregularities were present in Makati buildings (transfer girders, "mezzanine" floors, etc.) and plan irregularities (L-shaped buildings) existed in both cities; more RCMRF models should be developed to assign more appropriate vulnerabilities to different building classes.

### Vulnerability Assessment and Loss Estimation

**Repair Costs and Downtime:** Repair costs may be underestimated; estimates should be confirmed with DepEd/DPWH and local contractors, and high values of cost uncertainty should be further propagated. North American delay distributions used for downtime may be invalid, particularly for financing; calibration with empirical data from DPWH/DepEd from past earthquakes is needed. Alternative downtime functions should be developed. Re-Occupancy may not be a meaningful decision variable; Philippine-specific Repair Class definitions should be investigated.

**Component-Specific Work:** More elements should be considered including shelves (using peak floor velocities), science lab hazardous materials, and kitchen equipment. Concrete hollow block (CHB) walls with windows/doors should be changed from "glazing" to "partitions-exterior" categorization. CHB retrofitting solutions should continue to be developed with material suppliers; initial estimates with welded-wire-mesh are quite expensive and alternative FRP solutions show promise.

**Fatalities and Intensity Measures:** Fatality rates may be underestimated; investigation of increased collapse fatality rates from past studies is warranted. Spectral acceleration may not be an adequate intensity measure; the use of alternative intensity measures like average spectral acceleration should be investigated, and these should also be used for ground motion selection of distributed portfolios.

**Cost-Benefit Analysis:** Complete detailed cost-benefit analysis is needed. "Green" metrics should be explored; P-58 includes a methodology to calculate embodied carbon and energy that could better inform life cycle cost analysis and possibly qualify for climate bonds.

### Portfolio-Wide Loss Estimation

**Scenarios and Spatial Effects:** Investigation of subcrustal rupture scenarios is recommended. Spatial correlation assumptions should be reviewed as potentially invalid; sensitivity analysis with available spatial correlation functions should be conducted. Labour allocation at the portfolio-level should be investigated separately from building-level considerations.

**General Results and Community Resilience:** Results should be confirmed with other portfolio loss estimation platforms such as OpenQuake. Archetypal cost-benefit analysis should be extended to assess portfolio-level mitigation strategies. Community resilience and interdependent systems should be investigated using platforms like IN-CORE, recognizing that disaster impact is not limited to individual buildings but extends to broader infrastructure systems.

## 8.3 Recommendations to Stakeholders

The following section summarizes recommendations that will be communicated to Makati and Quezon City, DepEd, and DPWH officials.

### Short-Term Non-Structural Mitigations

Non-structural mitigations are possible in the short term and can be integrated with upcoming architectural renovations that the cities have planned. Ceiling upgrades would be accomplished by adding additional bracing. It is recommended to phase out pendant fixtures and examine the anchorage of fans and lights with the goal of upgrading with safety wires, wherever necessary. Additionally, it would be beneficial to move these fixtures from moment frame beams where cracking is likely to occur. Desktop electronics can be secured with safety straps or heavy-duty glue/tape. Anchorage of wall mounted air-conditioning units should be checked and upgraded with additional brackets where necessary. Shelves were not considered in the current thesis, but should be moved away from classroom exits if not anchored to the walls. 

The author will continue to work with Sika Philippines to propose a cheaper and more rapid solution for CHB retrofits, replacing the scheme with welded-wire-mesh that was proposed in Chapter 6. It is recommended to first retrofit walls in the stairwells due to the high exposure potential of occupants to falling CHB. Other masonry parapet-like elements (e.g., arches above building entrances) should be scanned to determine reinforcement details and removed if they are deemed unsafe.

Another short-term measure that is strongly suggested is that non-engineered buildings, particularly wooden Gabaldon (heritage, W-L), informal (N-L), and unreinforced masonry (CHB-L) structures, should be utilized for purposes other than classroom space. This would limit the exposure of the student population.

Other hazards seen during the 2018 rapid visual screening should be investigated in the short term and addressed in the medium term. It was noted that some emergency exits and stairs were locked or blocked in some other way, potentially leading to impediments to the egress routes should occupants attempt to evacuate in the event of an emergency. Damage from past earthquakes even as recent as the 2019 Mindanao events showed that unreinforced perimeter CHB walls and campus gate arches collapsed, which could cause severe injuries to students evacuating the premises.

### Medium- to Long-Term Structural Mitigations

It is strongly suggested officials continue ongoing efforts to replace collapse-prone 1-2-Storey Pre/Low-Code RCMRF buildings (C1-L (Pre-Lo)), which is of greater importance to Quezon City. As the World Bank retrofitting program continues to address high-risk campuses by the West Valley Fault, this thesis can serve as an additional source of information towards optimizing mitigation measures across the rest of the portfolio.

Initial estimates for FRP retrofitting of the 3-Storey RCMRF archetype (C1-M (Pre/Lo)) were estimated in this thesis at 35% of full replacement cost. This solution would need to be accompanied by rebar scanning and input from DPWH consultants, but could be rapidly adopted for buildings with the highest collapse risk. This may also reduce the need for detailed design of each building. While FRP shear retrofitting may significantly decrease the collapse risk, the derivation of fragilities suggested that the retrofit scheme may still result in high residual drifts. The building may still need to be condemned after an earthquake. Other structural schemes should be investigated for all RCMRFs, with a primary focus on reducing residual drifts and increasing resilience. These retrofits could be introduced incrementally from the mid- to long-term.

### Future Design Approaches

Another solution would involve applying a Philippine-specific resilience-based design approach like the one proposed in this thesis during the design of new school buildings. More research may go into considering whether post-tensioned RCMRFs present in the Makati building stock are, in fact, better at reducing post-earthquake residual drifts. Additionally, other metrics of disaster resilience could be studied, such as backup green energy supplies to complement rainwater capture systems already being implemented in newer designs.
