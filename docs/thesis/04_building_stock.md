# Chapter 4: Makati and Quezon City Public-School Building Stock

> **Source:** Jeswani, K.K. (2021). *The Seismic Resilience of Critical Spatially-Distributed Building Portfolios*. MASc thesis, University of Toronto. Printed pages 36–64 (PDF pages 63–91).

## 4.0 Overview

This chapter examines the target building portfolio exposed to the seismic hazards outlined in Chapter 3, by providing an overview of typical urban typologies found in Metro Manila (MM), regional construction practices, and general hazards associated with this infrastructure. Specific information on public-school infrastructure is then provided as an introduction to the more detailed data gathering and field study components. The procedure used Rapid Visual Screening (RVS) to enhance the cities' high-level public-school inventories and to refine the exposure database for use in the portfolio seismic risk assessment.

A significant portion of information used in defining the exposure database required input from subject matter experts and government authorities. Personal communications are cited throughout and referenced in Tables P-1 and P-2 (Preface) and Tables R-1 and R-2 (References).

## 4.1 Target Building Portfolio and Urban Context

Metro Manila is home to the two target cities — Makati and Quezon City — in addition to 14 other cities and one municipality. Out of the 13 million people residing in Metro Manila, an estimated 1.3 million are informal settlers (World Bank, 2017; PSA, 2018b). The Greater Metro Manila Area (GMMA) incorporates another 2.7 million informal settlers from surrounding provinces (Bautista M.L. et al., 2014). Approximately 31% of the GMMA's 20 million inhabitants live in densely packed neighbourhoods accounting for less than 9% of the total residential floor area. The region is highly exposed to natural hazards and is more vulnerable due to precarious and congested infrastructure (JICA et al., 2004).

### 4.1.1 Urban Typologies in Metro Manila

Bautista et al. (2014) developed a high-level building exposure database for the GMMA, classifying structural archetypes using the HAZUS-based UPD-ICE classification system. Key typologies relevant to public-school buildings, by seismic force resisting system (SFRS), are summarized in **Table 4-1**:

| UPD-ICE ID | Height Class | Primary Structural Components | Other Typical Properties |
|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | L, M, H | Cast-in-place (CIP) moment resisting space frame (RCMRF) | Slabs: CIP RC, precast RC, or steel deck & RC |
| C2 | L, M, H | CIP RC shear walls (RCSW) in both principal axes | Steel or wood truss roofs w/ galvanized iron (GI) sheets |
| C4 | L, M, H | RCSW & RCMRF dual system — one in each direction or combinations | Concrete hollow block (CHB) infill walls |
| CWS | L | RCMRF with upper storey in wood sheathing or light steel framing | Wood frame/sheathing floors; Light steel or wood truss roofs w/ GI sheets; CHB walls |
| PC1 | L | Precast tilt-up RCSWs | Wood frame/sheathing roofs or steel deck |
| PC2 | L | Precast RCMRF w/ or w/o precast/CIP RCSWs | Precast floor and/or roof diaphragms; CIP RC joints with welded steel inserts |
| MWS | L | Similar to CWS, but CHB walls instead of RCMRFs | Light steel or wood truss roofs w/ GI sheets |
| CHB | L | CHB walls | — |
| S1 | M | Steel moment resisting space frame (SMRF) | Steel deck & RC slabs; Steel roof truss with GI |
| S2 | L | Steel braced frame | Steel truss roof with GI |
| S3 | L | Prefabricated transverse rigid light steel frame and longitudinal tension-only rod bracing and/or wall sheathing panels | Bolted steel joints; GI roof on light steel framing/truss; CHB or other masonry infill walls |
| W1/2 | L | Wood frame and plywood/fiberboard wall sheathing | Wood frame/sheathing roofs or GI; W2 is >500 m² |
| N | L | Makeshift light wood frames and plywood, cardboard, tarpaulin, or GI sheet walls; GI roofs | — |

*Note: Low-rise (L) = 1–2 storeys; mid-rise (M) = 3–7 storeys; high-rise (H) = 7–15 storeys.*

Fosholt et al. (2018) noted that residential construction in dense neighbourhoods by the West Valley Fault is characterized by: non-observance of ductile structural detailing (e.g., stirrup spacing, code-compliant plastic hinge design); irregularities such as soft-storey conditions; building slenderness; offset columns; inadequate design of transfer girders; inadequate reinforcing for infill walls; and lack of seismic gaps between buildings resulting in pounding. Proper use of the National Structural Code of the Philippines (NSCP) is not always enforced due to lack of time and expertise. Non-engineered poorly-reinforced/un-reinforced concrete hollow block (CHB) buildings are extremely common.

Critical infrastructure — government buildings, police and fire stations, and public schools in the Philippines — is typically designed to the same standard as conventional structures, endangering post-disaster functionality (World Bank, 2014b).

### 4.1.2 Makati and Quezon City Public School Building Portfolio

This thesis presents a seismic risk assessment of a spatially distributed building portfolio of **over 1,000 public-school buildings** occupied by over 560,000 students across two cities in Metro Manila, Philippines. This includes:

- **Makati City:** 96 buildings across 40 elementary/secondary school campuses; one university campus; one DepEd office — distributed across 26 km².
- **Quezon City:** approximately 925 buildings across 142 schools — distributed across 165 km².

### 4.1.3 Past Studies on Public School Buildings in Metro Manila

The Department of Public Works and Highways (Bureau of Design, DPWH-BOD) often designs or reviews public building designs to ensure adherence to national codes (JICA et al., 2004). Earlier school building standard drawings were developed in 1984 and 1994 (Miyamoto et al., 2014). However, only few buildings with shear-walls were actually built in Metro Manila (Lopez, 2018–2020). Buildings owned by local governmental units (LGUs) could also be designed by in-house engineers (e.g., Quezon City Special Design Group, QC-SDG).

The DPWH oversaw a 2011 RVS of 816 school buildings in MM, of which less than 1% were in Makati and 12% in Quezon City (Lopez, 2018–2020). The study team noted that MM school buildings constituted similar typologies, mainly RCMRF with CHB infill (C1). These results were consistent with the MMEIRS study (JICA et al., 2004), Miyamoto and Gilani (2015), and the GMMA-RAP study (Bautista M.L. et al., 2014). The GMMA-RAP study broadly categorized all public schools in MM as: **95.23% C1, 4.43% CWS, 0.17% W1, and 0.17% W2.**

The 2010–2011 DPWH RVS classified 35% as C1 and an additional 60% broadly as "RC" (possibly with RCSWs as C2 or C4). The balance were wood frames (W1/W2), reinforced/un-reinforced CHB, steel moment (S1), steel braced frames (S2), steel moment frames with CHB infill (S5), and combinations thereof. As many as **86% of school buildings screened had FEMA-154 (2002) scores below the proposed cut-off of 2.0**, indicating a greater collapse probability and need for detailed evaluation.

## 4.2 Data Collection

Data collection on available public-school building inventories and other relevant information is summarized in **Table 4-2** (see thesis). Key data sources included:

**Makati City:**
- Makati GSD (2018): School building inventory as of 2018 — construction dates, number of stories, number of rooms, approximate floor areas, addresses; structural drawings of 10 buildings (1999–2009); geotechnical reports of four sites; site development plans for 30 school sites; general floor plans for 21 buildings.
- Makati UDD (2018): Architectural drawings of 55 buildings.
- DepEd Makati (2018): 2017–2018 student enrolment per school.
- Makati DRRMO (2018): Hazard maps for individual school sites.

**Quezon City:**
- DepEd QC DRRMO (2018): Public school building inventory as of 2013 — building name/funding source, number of storeys, total floor area, total rooms, room size, date of completion, address, number of bathrooms.
- DepEd QC INFRA (2018): Site development plans (140 of 143 schools) — buildings' name/type, plan areas, number of storeys, number of rooms.
- QC SDG (2018): List of 22 new buildings (2011–2016) and structural drawings for 1 RCMRF and 1 Steel-MRF.
- QC DRRMO: Hazard maps (GIS files) — liquefaction potential, building damage estimation, casualty estimation, flood susceptibility.

**Additional sources:** DPWH (2018) — 1994 RCMRF and RC-SW structural drawings; drawings of 7 other buildings from the 1950s–1990s. DepEd EFD (2018) — earlier school building standard drawings (2007–2014). Subject matter experts: Lopez (2018–2020), ACOng (2018), SY² (2018) — local construction and design practices.

Prior to altering input inventory data, the rapid visual screening described in Section 4.3 was conducted.

## 4.3 Rapid Visual Screening

To supplement the high-level building inventories, a Rapid Visual Screening (RVS) was conducted in Makati and Quezon City as per **FEMA P-154 (2015)** in early 2018. Vertical and horizontal irregularities, redundancies in SFRS, possibility of pounding, and other potentially detrimental features were noted.

**Limitations noted:**
- Time constraints hindered detailed investigation of structural deficiencies and interior hazards.
- Limited access to certain buildings due to ongoing classes or restricted access.
- Unknown presence of seismic gaps made it difficult to determine whether building blocks were structurally attached.
- Less time was spent screening buildings that appeared identical to previously screened ones (based on construction date, building name, and quick visual comparisons).

Additional RVS results are presented in Appendix B.3 in terms of archetypes defined in Section 4.4.

### 4.3.1 Rapid Visual Screening – Makati City

RVS was conducted on **53 (55%) out of 96 Makati public-school buildings**, with the main purpose of identifying primary building typologies and classifying inventory entries.

**Rebar scanning** of five buildings (Figure 4-3) was conducted with the assistance of engineers from SY² (2018) to differentiate between reinforced-concrete shear-walls (RCSWs) and CHB walls in older buildings. This led to ruling out the use of DPWH 1994 and 1984 school building standards in the definition of index buildings (referenced by Miyamoto et al., 2014). These standards are equivalent to HAZUS-based UPD-ICE C4-L and C4-M typologies (reinforced concrete frame-wall dual systems).

An attempt was made to distinguish between wall types by auditory inspection in remaining suspected buildings (knocking to estimate hollowness). This was largely unsuccessful due to the possibility of some CHB walls being fully grouted.

**Key findings:**
- Only **two 2-storey buildings were confirmed to have RCSWs** by rebar scanning.
- Most other screened buildings were assigned two possible archetypes: C1 or C4, with C1 being the most probable (Lopez, 2018–2020).
- Architecturally, Makati public-school buildings were relatively uniform — typical mid-rise and low-rise buildings are primarily RCMRFs.
- Modern buildings have post-tensioned moment frame beams (PTB) (Makati GSD, 2018).
- **Two high-rise RCMRFs (10–11 storey)** are part of the Makati inventory but were not screened.

### 4.3.2 Rapid Visual Screening – Quezon City

RVS was conducted on **129 (14%) of 932 buildings** (as of January 2018). Clustered campuses with the largest number of buildings were targeted first; additional campuses were added to gather information on a wider range of typologies.

**Key challenges:**
- Rebar scanning was not conducted for QC buildings, creating greater difficulty in determining which buildings followed DPWH 1994/1984 standards.
- "Most probable" and "possible" archetypes were recorded for all screened buildings.
- The provided QC inventory "Construction Type" descriptors were deemed inaccurate (e.g., "Semi-Concrete", "Prefab", "Makeshift", "Wood", "Gabaldon").

**Key findings:**
- RCMRFs were the most common structural system found, consistent with Makati.
- "Concrete" construction-type buildings appeared to be RCMRFs with CHB infill (C1).
- New steel moment frame (S1-M) structures with concrete-encased columns and CHB infill were also observed ("HB Hall" and "SB Hall" type buildings).
- Buildings were grouped into archetypes strongly based on architectural features and building names, as many buildings with the same name/type appeared to share the same layout.

## 4.4 Data Processing

### 4.4.1 Identification of Applicable Construction Eras

Construction "code eras" were established before sorting building inventory entries. Based on the works of UPD-ICE (Pacheco et al., 2014; Tingatinga E. et al., 2019) and subject matter experts (Lopez, 2018–2020), the author developed a **code era sorting matrix** for classifying entries and further breaking down structural typologies.

**Date handling conventions:**
- **Makati:** Construction *start* date used for sorting. If absent, completion date used under assumption that construction started one year prior.
- **Quezon City:** Construction *completion* date used for sorting.
- A **two-year lag period** was assumed between a building's construction start date and the corresponding NSCP edition implemented (Lopez, 2018–2020).

**Table 4-3: Code Era Sorting Matrix**

| Code Era | Pre-Code ("Pre") | Low-Code ("Lo") | Mid-Code ("Mid") | High-Code ("Hi") |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| NSCP Edition | Pre-1972 | 1972, 1982, 1986 | 1992 | 2001, 2010, 2015 |
| Makati — Construction Start Date | Before 1974 | 1974–1993 | 1994–2002 | 2003+ |
| Makati & QC — Construction Completion Date | Before 1975 | 1975–1994 | 1995–2003 | 2004+ |

Details on structural and seismic detailing differences between code editions are provided in Appendix C.1. Buildings with unknown construction dates in QC were labelled "UNK" and ignored in the final analysis.

### 4.4.2 Inventory Processing – Makati City

**Eight fields** were available in the provided Makati public-school building inventories:
(i) construction start/completion date, (ii) number of stories, (iii) approximate floor areas per building, (iv) number of academic classrooms, (v) number of instructional rooms, (vi) number of function rooms, (vii) approximate lot areas, (viii) school campus addresses.

Key transformations (Table 4-4):
- Building entries separated into multiple buildings where seismic gaps were found (from drawings or RVS).
- Buildings named "4-Storey w/ Mezzanine Bldg II" treated as 5-storey buildings.
- Plan shape determined from Google Earth and drawings (see Appendix B.2.1 for examples).
- Missing building floor areas estimated from structural drawings or by dividing campus floor area proportionally.
- Building population derived as: `= (Campus Population) × (BFA / CFA)`.
- Street addresses geocoded via ARCGIS (ESRI, 2019).
- RVS observations appended to the processed building inventory for stakeholder release.

**Distribution (Figure 4-9):**
- Most common plan shape: **rectangular (73%)**.
- Most significant portion of building stock: 3- to 5-storey (mid-rise) RCMRFs with code era distribution:
  - Low-Code: **22%**
  - Mid-Code: **42%**
  - High-Code: **24%**

### 4.4.3 Inventory Processing – Quezon City

**Twelve fields** were available in the original Quezon City building inventories:
(i) building name, (ii) construction type, (iii) construction completion date, (iv) number of stories, (v) ground floor footprint area, (vi) number of instructional/non-instructional rooms, (vii) number of comfort rooms, (viii) total floor areas, (ix) typical room floor area, (x) funding source, (xi) contract amount per building, (xii) campus address.

**Pre-processing:** A manual update of 140 provided school building entries was conducted using 2016–2017 site development plans (Appendix B.2.2) and the list of 22 new buildings (2011–2016) from QC SDG (2018).

**Demolitions:** Approximately **139 buildings** (90 of which are 1-storey) were demolished between 2013–2017 (based on entries noted as such or absent from updated site plans). The remainder of the portfolio consisted of approximately **932 school buildings in Quezon City** as of January 2018.

Covered courts (e.g., open-air SMRF, steel braced frames) were not included in this building inventory and were considered outside scope.

Key transformations (Table 4-5):
- Total Floor Area (TFA) in original data was actually the building footprint area; corrected: `BFA = TFA × Storeys`.
- Contract amounts of 111 buildings processed; lognormal distributions fit for 98 "Concrete" buildings (median eμ = ₱19,800/m², dispersion β = 0.29) and 13 "Steel" buildings (median eμ = ₱30,500/m², β = 0.20) — for buildings built 2010+ with inflation adjustment.
- Building population derived as: `= (Campus Population) × (BFA / ΣBFAper campus)`.
- Rectangular plan-shaped buildings represented **95% of the total building stock** (Figure 4-10).
- Significant portions of 3- to 4-storey (mid-rise) Mid-Code (17%) and High-Code (32%) structures; significant concentration of older (Pre-/Low-Code) 1- to 2-storey (low-rise) buildings.

### 4.4.4 Selection of Representative Index Buildings for Makati City

Once the preliminary Makati inventory was assembled, archetypes were assigned to screened buildings, then extrapolated for the remaining inventory.

**Key assumption:** Most 4- and 5-storey (and possibly 3-storey) Makati school buildings are PTMRFs (post-tensioned moment frame beams), more accurate for buildings constructed from 1993 onwards (Makati GSD, 2018). Buildings with longitudinal column spacing of 4 m (vs. 8 m for Mid-/High-Code) were classified as non-PT Low-Code.

**Table 4-6: Summary of Makati Public-School Building Archetypes**

| UPD-ICE Code / Stories | Archetype ID | Modelled in Ch.6? | System | Sorting Reasoning & Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1-L Pre-Low (1–2) | C1-L (Pre/Lo) | YES, Simplified | RCMRF (with CHB infill) | Construction started pre-1993 |
| C1-L Mid-High (1–2) | C1-L (Mid/Hi) | YES | RCMRF (with CHB infill) | Construction 1994+ |
| C1-M Pre-Low (3–5) | C1-M (Pre/Lo) | YES | RCMRF (with CHB infill) | Construction started pre-1993; buildings with longitudinal column spacing of 4 m vs. 8 m (drawings & RVS). Otherwise → PTC1-M (LC) |
| C1-M (w/ PTB?) Pre (4) | PTC1-M (Pre/Lo) | No, Merged | RCMRF (with CHB infill) | No structural drawings — possibly with PTB. RVS: large spans & shallow girders |
| C1-M (w/ PTB) Low (3–5) | PTC1-M (Pre/Lo) | No, Merged | RCMRF (with CHB infill) | Low-Code, but construction starting 1993/94 PTMRFs (Makati GSD, 2018) |
| C1-M (w/ PTB) Mid (3–5) | PTC1-M (Mid) | YES — (via proxy) | RCMRF (with CHB infill) & PTB | Mid-Code, PTMRFs (Makati GSD, 2018) |
| C1-M (w/ PTB) High (3–6) | PTC1-M (Hi) | YES — (via proxy) | RCMRF (with CHB infill) & PTB | Available structural drawings and confirmed by Makati GSD (2018) |
| C4-L Low-Mid (2) | C4-L (Lo/Mid) | No, Merged | RCMRF (with CHB Infill) transverse SWs | Confirmed RCSW by rebar scanning |
| C4-M (w/ PTB) Low (4) | PTC4-M (Lo) | No, Merged | RCMRF (with CHB Infill), PTB, and elevator core RCSW | Available structural drawings; only 1 building |
| C1-H High (10–11) | C1-H (Hi) | Yes, Simplified | RCMRF w/ basement | Available structural drawings |

**Groupings and rationale:**
- Low-rise RCMRFs (C1-L) grouped into Pre-/Low-Code and Mid-/High-Code combinations, as differences in seismic response were expected to be marginal (consistent with UPD-ICE classification, Bautista M.L. et al., 2014).
- Pre- and Low-Code PTMRFs grouped together as non-ductile behavior expected to be similar; only one of four buildings is Pre-Code.
- Pre-Code and 1–2-storey buildings ear-marked for demolition in the short- and medium-term (Makati GSD, 2018).

**Floor area distribution (Figure 4-11 — Most Probable bound):**
- Mid-rise Mid- and High-Code PTMRFs (PTC1-M (Mid) and PTC1-M (Hi)): **~57% of total floor area** in Makati.
- Pre- and Low-Code PTMRFs (PTC1-M (Pre/Lo)): **~10–17%** (depending on classification of some buildings as C1-M (Pre/Lo)).
- C1-H (Hi) high-rise buildings: **~18% of total floor area** (only 2 buildings; one confirmed RCMRF from drawings).
- C1-L (Pre/Lo): ~1%; C1-M (Pre/Lo): ~14%; C1-L (Mid/Hi): ~0.3%; C4-L (Lo/Mid): ~0.4%

### 4.4.5 Selection of Representative Index Buildings for Quezon City

The grouping of Quezon City public-school buildings required more assumptions and engineering judgement than Makati City, attributed to greater variation in building typologies and the fact that screening covered only 14% of the building stock.

Two bounds ("most probable" and "possible") were recorded for archetypes, based on the possibility of buildings having RCSWs and other uncertainties.

**Key assumptions:**
- "Concrete" and "Semi-Concrete" buildings were in fact RCMRFs, as per observations of the 2010–11 DPWH RVS (Lopez, 2018–2020) and January 2018 RVS.
- Building type/name (e.g., "DepEd Building", "Mathay", "SB Hall", "HB Hall") was indicative of funding source and could provide insight into structural archetype, as one building design was often constructed on multiple campuses (Lopez, 2018–2020).

Screened buildings were classified into UPD-ICE-consistent archetypes; remaining buildings were extrapolated based on name, funding source, code era, stories, and number of rooms.

**Archetype distribution (Figure 4-13):**
- Newer and larger buildings, C1-M (Hi) and S1-M (Hi), accounted for **~47% of the QC building stock** (by count).
- The QC school building stock primarily consisted of RCMRFs, totaling **~73%**, with another possible **~5%** contributed by C4-M typology (1994 DPWH standards).
- City officials were strongly prioritizing building replacement by demolishing older 1- and 2-storey buildings.

"Most probable" vs. "possible" differences arise due to consideration of dual-system RCMRF/SW (C4) buildings and some small contributions by possible precast RC frames.

## 4.5 Concluding Remarks

This chapter described the methodology employed in defining building typologies for the case study building portfolio. Rapid visual screening played a key role in increasing the resolution of this building exposure database, as the provided high-level inventories lacked an appropriate structural classification system. An analysis of the archetype distributions in both cities allowed for decisions on modelling methods, including the merging of some archetypes with a small presence in the building stock. More detail on modelling decisions of these archetypes is presented in Chapter 5.
