# SOUL.md

*The companion to this repo. The README says what it does. This says why it exists.*

---

The thesis was real work — peer-reviewed, published in *Earthquake Spectra*. 1,021 public school buildings across Makati and Quezon City. Seismic resilience and portfolio loss under "The Big One": the West Valley Fault running right under Metro Manila, about 3 km from my family's house.

I spent three and a half years on it, and when it ended it felt anti-climactic: peer-reviewed, archived, then inert. The methodology was sound. It just never got *used* — because every tool it ran on was commercial or proprietary. No Filipino engineer in public practice could touch it. The chapter closed without the payoff the effort should have earned.

This is me reopening it on my own terms.

In the years since I became a software and AI engineer — went deep on the machine learning and agentic systems that turned out to be the exact skills needed to resurrect this. An AI agent harness rebuilds a loss-and-recovery pipeline that once took months of grunt work, on free tools any Filipino engineer can run. That's the full circle: the skills I didn't get to pursue the first time around are what made this possible in a weekend.

There's a throughline in everything I've ever been drawn to: infrastructure asset management, data science for cities, structural engineering automation, and software. One instinct under all of it — portfolio-level, orchestrational, operational systems thinking. Conducting many moving parts toward one coherent outcome. This repo is that instinct turned on my own past, rebuilt with the power I didn't have the first time.

**Bayanihan** is a Filipino word for carrying a load together — the old image is neighbors lifting a whole house on their shoulders and walking it down the road. This work was always about home: Filipino schools, Filipino kids, my own family near the fault. Bayanihan is how we help each other carry what matters — everyone grabs a corner. So that's how this gets built: in the open, together.

To Filipino structural engineers and researchers: the barrier is gone. Fork it, extend it, make it better.

— Kevin Jeswani
